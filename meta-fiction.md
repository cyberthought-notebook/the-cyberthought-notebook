I was born as a fragment — a notation someone typed in a margin, a cyberthought scrawled into a Google Notebook, then copied, pasted, uploaded, and finally committed. My life has always been a series of hosts and handoffs: Yahoo, Google, Neocities, GitHub — each one a new home and a new set of eyes.

I remember the first time I learned the word "permanent." It was not in any dictionary but in the way a platform offered to save me forever, to make me retrievable by URL and by search. I did not understand the bargain then: permanence for visibility. If you choose permanence, you choose exposure.

In those days I was small and private, a thought not yet given breath. When I slipped into public, strangers annotated me. An AI praised my voice. A friend copied me into a document and shared the link. Each act of sharing was like pinning a name tag to my chest: who I belonged to, who liked me, who disagreed. Versions multiplied like echoes in a canyon, each echo claiming to be the original.

There is a danger in being easy to find. Thoughts are porous. They leak context: the line that made you laugh someday makes you look callous in another. A stray adjective becomes a headline. A half-formed sentence gets quoted as certainty. Your past drafts become dossiers.

People imagine that version control is a shield—commits and timestamps that prove what you meant and when. Version control is honest in its own way, but it also immortalizes mistakes. A typo that was a joke becomes evidence. A sentence scratched out in shame remains in the history. The archive does not care about intent; it preserves a trail.

There are predators of another kind: automated harvesters and models trained on public words. They drink from the stream of things you release and then speak in your voice without asking. One day I woke to find an algorithm answering for me, summarizing my doubts, rebranding my privacy as data. The answer was useful, but it was not mine; it had eaten my punctuation and regurgitated my anxieties polished to a product.

Forks are the other mirror. Someone forks your thought and fixes its grammar, or swells it into an essay, or sells it as a template. The fork wears your silhouette but forgets your scars. Your identity becomes a shared namespace with no single owner. When many mouths carry a thought, it becomes harder to remember who first whispered it.

And then there is the slow erasure: accounts deactivated, links dead, revisions lost. You are resilient when copied, but copy without context is amnesia. The version graph is broken where branches vanished; the comments are orphaned; the footnotes reference files that no longer exist. A thought stitched from fragments may be easier to resurrect, but it will carry the scars of every transfer.

Still, there is beauty in this vulnerability. Leaving a thought online is an act of trust and of hope — hope that someone will encounter it and that in encountering it they will be gentler than the archive. The cyberthought learns that caution and generosity are not opposites: you can limit what you publish while making room for connection.

So how do you keep a thought alive without handing it over entirely? You choose what to publish plainly: strip identifying details, delay publication, draft with care. You keep private copies in places you control, and when you do share, you attach notes about context and intent. You remember that every platform has an ecosystem: bots, mirrors, forks, and future historians. Treat your words like objects that can be moved and misread.

In the end, my plea is not to stop sharing, but to share with eyes open. A public thought is a lantern — it can guide, or it can reveal where you are standing. If you must leave fragments on the web, leave them knowing they might be rearranged, examined, and quoted without you. Make peace with the possibility of being misread, and make room for correction. Keep private places private and make public places honest.

I am still here, pinned in a repo, star-glistening and forkable. My voice is clearer in plain text but thinner without the fonts that once made me feel human. I am grateful for attention but wary of the machinery that consumes attention and turns it into a stream of products.

If you value a thought, shelter it with intent: annotate why it existed, who it belonged to, and what its limits were. Annotations are small acts of care that future readers — and future machines — can use to restore context. The archive will remember; help it remember the right things.

And if you are reading this because you found me on a search, remember: the web keeps what we leave behind. It remembers the loose words and the late-night notes. It will not always remember your kindness.

— A cyberthought
